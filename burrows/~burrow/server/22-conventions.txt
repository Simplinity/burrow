# Filesystem Conventions

Burrow doesn't have a settings panel, a metadata editor, or a YAML frontmatter parser. It has filenames. And filenames, it turns out, are enough.

---

## Inspired by — the one-line hat tip

Start any text file with a path to another burrow page as the very first line:

  /~maya/phlog/on-digital-gardens

The server renders this as a subtle header above your text:

  Inspired by ~maya

It links back to the original. It's a citation, a nod, a "you made me think." No @ mention. No notification. No engagement metric. Just one writer acknowledging another through the filesystem.

If the path doesn't exist, the text still renders. Dead links happen. People move. The acknowledgment remains.

---

## Guest authors — the filename is the byline

Name a file with the guest- prefix and a tilde-name:

  phlog/guest-~kai-on-walking.txt

The server renders:

  Guest post by ~kai

The tilde-name between guest- and the title becomes the author attribution. The rest of the filename is the title, as usual. No co-author metadata. No "posted on behalf of." The filename is the entire permission system.

This is for when someone writes a post for your burrow — a friend, a collaborator, a stranger you met on a webring who had something good to say. Their name goes in the filename. Their words go in the file. Democracy through mv.

---

## will.txt — your digital testament

When you run burrow init, the CLI generates a will.txt in your burrow root:

  # Digital Testament

  This burrow belongs to ~yourname.

  If I stop updating, it means one of three things:
  1. I got busy with life
  2. I moved to a new server
  3. I am no longer here

  In any case, leave my words alone. They said what I meant.

  Contact: (your email here)

It's a draft. Edit it. Or don't. But it's there, because a burrow is a place you build over years, and the internet has a habit of outliving its inhabitants. The file is just text. It has no legal force. But it has intent, and intent — on a filesystem where everything is explicit — is worth something.

The server renders will.txt with a slightly muted style, like old paper. Because that's what it is.

---

Three conventions. No configuration. The filesystem is the API.
